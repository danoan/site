Bayesian Rational in Image Processing

Imagine you are going to receive some friends and you decide to prepare them a delicious dish whose recipe your learned in your last trip. Your friends love the food and they wonder what are the ingredients. They start to guess if that spicy feeling it comes from aji amarillo; some others are sure to have tasted cinnamon.

Prepare the dish is a forward problem, while guessing the ingredients it is an example of an inverse problem. Inverse problems are usually ill-posed, meaning that we may have multiple solutions or not solution at all. To regularise ill-posed problems we have to include assumptions to our models. In the example above, your guests could refine their guesses if you told them that all the ingredients came from south america, for example.

Image processing is a domain full of ill-posed problems, and in this post we describe how to tackle a classical one, image denoising, from the Bayesian point of view.

The tautochrone curve.

A track should be constructed to connect some point in space P to a lower altitude point Q. Which form the track should take if we wish that a ball released at P reaches Q in the shortest time? The curve known as tautochrone (or brachistochrone) is the answer of this puzzle solved by Jean Bernoulli and a classical problem of the calculus of variations.
